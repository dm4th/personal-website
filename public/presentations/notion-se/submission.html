Arcline's Monday EPD Digest.
Automated, cited, human-reviewed, and in the VP's inbox before standup.

Four data-source workers mirror GitHub, Jira, Slack, and Figma into Notion at 6 AM. Eight Custom Agents (each called once per squad) produce 18 source summaries across three squads.

Brief Video Intro
01 · The Problem

Verbatim, from Arcline's VP of Engineering

"Our engineering teams are spread across 8 squads with PMs and designers embedded in each. Every Monday, engineering managers spend 3 to 4 hours manually stitching together sprint status from Jira, GitHub PRs, Slack threads, and design review notes in Figma just to produce a weekly EPD digest for leadership. By the time it reaches the VP level, it's already outdated and missing context. We'd like to use AI to automate this, but our leadership makes resourcing decisions based on this digest. If the AI hallucinates a status update or misinterprets a PR's intent, that's a real problem. How do we trust the output?" — VP Engineering, Arcline Technologies

The trust question is the hard problem. Summarization is solved. Knowing when to trust a summary is not. Every design decision in this build traces back to that question.

02 · Before Designing Anything

The five questions I'd ask Arcline

These aren't process theater. Each answer changes the architecture.

  1. 1
    In what format do VPs prefer to read this report, and how often do they request ad-hoc updates?
    The format question determines whether I build a Notion page, a Slack digest, or a PDF export. The frequency question tells me if a Monday cadence is enough or if VPs want on-demand queries. Knowing this before designing the output layer prevents an expensive rebuild at week three.
    Structured notion page with specific callouts for Highlights, Responses Feedback from Last Meeting, Risks & BLockers, Cross-Squad Dependencies, Roadmap Changes, Open Discrepancies
  2. 2
    Which data source is the most consistently structured, and which is the hardest to parse?
    I want to scope the POC around the highest-value, lowest-risk source first. If Jira is clean and Slack is messy, I lead with Jira and build trust before tackling the noisy sources. This question determines Week 1 scope more than anything else in the 30-day plan.
    GitHub and Jira as the most well-structured and more of the source of truth compared to both Slack and Figma. Figma is more of a source of truth than Slack, but is also much messier. I do not address the complexity of cleaning each of these data sources individually in my submission.
  3. 3
    Which part of the Monday stitching process takes engineering managers the most time?
    Time savings is the KPI I'm pitching. If the bottleneck is pulling data from GitHub vs. writing the cross-squad narrative, the architecture priorities change. I need to know where the 3-4 hours actually goes before claiming I can reclaim it. Solving the wrong bottleneck first costs a week.
    Gathering the data from each data source and creating linkages and citations back to the source of truth is what I am assuming to be the largest lift from a time perspective.
  4. 4
    What is your tolerance for a human-in-the-loop review gate, and is the goal to remove it eventually?
    The HITL gate is load-bearing for trust in V1, but it also consumes EM time. If leadership needs full autonomy by day 90, I architect the confidence-measurement infrastructure differently than if HITL is acceptable indefinitely. The answer shapes how much investment we make now in the eval harness vs. the approval workflow.
    I assume that early HITL is acceptable, but that the customer would be willing to pay a little more over time in AI credits to have the system fully automated.
  5. 5
    Two questions I'd ask here. I include both because each catches a failure mode the other misses:
    5a: Do the 8 squads share a consistent definition of "done" across Jira, GitHub, and Figma, or does each squad use these tools differently? Inconsistent tool hygiene is the hardest problem for an AI to handle gracefully. If Atlas closes Jira tickets on merge but Forge closes them on deploy, the conflict-detection logic is completely different. I need to know this before writing a single agent prompt.
    I assume that the definition of done varies widely across the 3 squads I mocked. Highlighting the inconsistencies between tool usage across tools and squads is something this system should correct for.


    5b: What does success look like for the engineering managers who are currently doing this work? Are they bought in, or should I expect friction? This is the change management question I almost didn't ask. If EMs see this as a threat to their visibility, adoption stalls at the HITL gate. If they're eager to reclaim Monday mornings, the POC accelerates. The answer shapes how I communicate the solution's value to the team, not just to the VP.
    Engineering managers are tech savvy, and are very much willing participants to be brought into the loop. This is especially true during the POC and early deployment phases.

The architecture solves the trust problem through reduction of context per agent, not expansion of it. Instead of one all-knowing summarizer reading 4 data sources across 8 squads, there are 8 Custom Agents each with a narrow mandate: one source type. Each agent runs once per squad, producing 18 source summaries across 3 squads and 6 source types. Each can be evaluated, tuned, or replaced independently. The master synthesizer reads pre-validated summaries, not raw data.

Workers run twice daily (6 AM and 6 PM) as a continuous data sync; no agents fire, no notifications are sent, they just keep the Mirror Databases fresh. On Monday at 7 AM, a cron triggers the source summarizer agents (each of the 6 source-type agents runs once per squad), for 18 total summary runs across the 3 squads. Each one computes its own target week at runtime by reading the current date, so no trigger row or coordinator is needed. This cron-based model replaced an earlier event-driven design where workers wrote Agent Run Log rows that immediately woke agents, which caused agents to fire before any human had decided to proceed, and created wasted "ghost" evaluation calls from non-matching agents polling every trigger row.

The Mirror Databases are load-bearing. By writing every source record into structured Notion DBs before any summarization happens, citations become queryable: an auditor can trace any claim in the master report back to the exact source row in the mirror. This two-hop citation chain (master → squad summary → mirror record → original source) is what makes the 81% Trust Score computable at all.

The conflict-resolution policy is explicit and customer-editable: Jira wins for status, GitHub wins for code facts, Figma wins for design decisions, Slack is flagged but never treated as authoritative. When two sources disagree, the agent doesn't guess. It surfaces the conflict in an Open Discrepancies section with both pieces of evidence and the ruling. The VP sees the disagreement, not a fabricated consensus.

Design correction (from first full run-through): The original event-driven trigger model had two failure modes discovered during a live W19 demo. (1) Workers wrote agent trigger rows that caused Notion Custom Agents to start running immediately, before generate-summaries.ts could write anything, and before any human had approved proceeding. (2) Notion's Custom Agent polling model means every agent evaluates every trigger row, even rows meant for other agents. With 12 trigger rows created per week and multiple agents watching the log, most evaluations do nothing and exit; wasted API calls show up as Outcome: skipped entries in the run log. The fix: workers write data only. Agent scheduling moves to a Monday 7 AM cron entirely separate from the data-ingestion path.
03b · Other Paths Considered

Why 8 focused agents instead of 1 (or 3)

Three simpler architectures were evaluated and rejected before settling on the current design. Each trades complexity for a failure mode that surfaces at scale.

Path 1: Rejected
Single master agent: one agent reads all sources and writes the report

The simplest architecture: one agent ingests GitHub, Jira, Slack, and Figma across all squads simultaneously and writes the VP report directly. Easiest to configure, hardest to trust. The context window must hold all source data for all squads at once, so cross-squad facts blur together and tracking which claim came from which squad's data becomes opaque. When output is wrong, there's no intermediate artifact to inspect or tune. And because the agent handles everything, there's no natural HITL insertion point per source or per squad.

Path 2: Rejected
One summarizer per squad + master (4 agents total)

Each of the 3 squads gets one agent that reads all 4 data sources for that squad; a master then synthesizes across squads. More tractable than Path 1, but a single squad agent still has to reconcile conflicting signals across sources without a per-source layer that EMs can inspect. When Atlas's agent flags a GitHub/Jira status conflict, the EM must read the entire squad summary to find it, rather than reviewing a targeted GitHub vs. Jira comparison. Tuning is also harder: a bad squad summary could stem from bad GitHub parsing, bad Jira parsing, or a reasoning error in cross-source reconciliation, with no way to isolate which without re-running everything.

Path 3: Rejected
Per-source summarizers + direct master: skip squad consolidation

Keep the 12 per-source summarizers but feed the master directly from all 12, removing the 3 squad consolidation agents. One fewer agent tier, but the master now inherits every unresolved cross-source conflict from all squads at once: the same context-sprawl risk as Path 1, just one level down. The squad consolidation step is where cross-source conflicts are resolved at squad scope (GitHub says merged, Jira says in progress) before they reach the master. Without it, the master synthesizes from 12 potentially-conflicting inputs across 3 squads with no intermediate reconciliation pass.

If the current layered architecture doesn't produce immediately trustworthy results, the eval harness provides the objective signal to compare builds over time, not a gut call made up front.

When an EM clicks Approve on a summary, the agent writes an approval record to the Delivery Pipeline database. The master summarizer polls this; once all three squads have approved all six summaries, it fires automatically. No manual trigger, no Slack message, no meeting.

If an EM clicks Reject, they annotate what's wrong, and the per-source summarizer re-runs with the rejection comment as additional context. This HITL feedback loop is also the training signal: over time, rejection patterns reveal which sources or agent prompts need tuning, and the eval harness measures whether prompt changes improve or regress the scores.

The approval count compression: 18 individual source summaries become 3 squad-level approvals, which then become 1 master approval. An EM reading a pre-written, cited, 500-word summary takes ~15 minutes per source, not the 90 minutes it takes to write one from scratch. That compression is where the EM time savings actually comes from.

08 · Top 3 Risks

What I'm most worried about, and what I did about it

Risk 1: High
Agent Sprawl: 8 agents across 18 weekly runs is a lot for a customer to own after handoff

The multi-agent architecture exists for a good reason: smaller context per agent means fewer hallucinations, independent tunability, and clear blast radius when something goes wrong. But asking Arcline's engineering team to maintain 8 agent prompts, 6 workers, and a custom eval harness after a 30-day POC handoff is a non-trivial ask. If the SE team doesn't set Arcline up to fish, this becomes a support dependency forever.

What I did about it: Two mitigations, both live. First, the Teaching Layer (PRD-10): every database and agent has an embedded explainer page in the workspace itself: "What this is," "Who writes to it," "How to change the agent prompt." An Arcline EM can learn the system without reading a doc. Second, the Observability Dashboard (PRD-07): the Agent Run Log surfaces run latency, token cost, and error rate per agent in a live Notion view. Sprawl becomes measurable; problems become visible before they become outages.

What would change my mind: If Arcline's engineering organization already has a strong internal tooling team that maintains custom automations, the handoff risk is lower. If it doesn't, the V2 roadmap should prioritize a "prompt management UI": a Notion page where an EM can edit agent prompts without touching code.

Risk 2: Medium
Data Staleness: the report reflects a Monday-morning snapshot, not live state

The workers run at 6 AM and write a snapshot. By the time the VP reads the master report at 10 AM, some of those records may already be outdated: a PR merged, a blocker resolved, a Jira ticket updated. The 3-4 hour manual process has the same problem, but it's implicit. Making it explicit in an AI-generated report can feel like a regression even if it isn't.

What I did about it: Staleness is flagged, not fixed. Every mirror record carries a Last Updated timestamp. The Open Discrepancies section calls out records that haven't been updated in more than N hours relative to report generation time. The master report explicitly notes its data coverage window. The VP sees: "Data as of 6:00 AM · 2 records flagged as potentially stale."

The V2 plan: Continuous workers on a 30-minute cron through Monday afternoon, updating mirror records as sources change. The master report generation becomes triggered by "all approved + data freshness within 2 hours" rather than a fixed time. This deferred to V2 because the trust problem has to be solved at the point-in-time snapshot level first; continuous staleness detection on top of an untrusted base report adds complexity without adding confidence.

What would change my mind: If Arcline's VPs are making time-sensitive resourcing decisions mid-Monday (not end-of-day), continuous workers move from V2 to V1. The question "how often do you need the report to be fresh during the day?" should be asked before Week 1 kickoff.

Risk 3: Low
AI Token Cost: 23 weekly API calls is a recurring line item, not a one-time build cost

Every Monday the pipeline makes approximately 23 model calls: 18 source-summarizer runs, 3 squad consolidations, 1 master synthesis, and 1 eval judge pass. At current Claude pricing this is a small number — roughly $1–3 per weekly run depending on context size — but it's a perpetual cost that scales with squad count. If Arcline expands from 3 squads to 8, the run count grows proportionally, and leadership may want to revisit the frequency or scope before approving that expansion.

Already tracked: The Observability Dashboard (PRD-07) logs token cost per run in the Agent Run Log. Weekly pipeline spend is visible in a live Notion rollup without any additional tooling. If cost becomes a concern, the levers are clear: reduce agent run frequency (bi-weekly instead of weekly), make the Roadmap and PRD Fact Checker agents truly opt-in rather than default, or use a lighter model (Haiku instead of Sonnet) for the source summarizers while keeping Sonnet for master synthesis. None of these require architectural changes.

Why it's low: The EM time savings alone — conservatively 6 EM-hours per week at a fully-loaded rate — dwarf the model cost by two orders of magnitude. Cost only becomes a conversation if token pricing increases significantly or Arcline's AI budget is constrained org-wide, neither of which is signaled in the prompt. Worth monitoring; not worth designing around pre-emptively.

09 · 30-Day POC Plan

Week-by-week, with real success criteria

Success at 30 days: a fully agentic pipeline generates a master report by 10 AM on at least 2 consecutive Mondays, with Trust Score ≥ 75%, without an SE in the loop. The 11 AM floor (VP quote) is the minimum; 10 AM is the target.

Week 1
May 20–24

Data plane + schema

Bootstrap the Notion workspace schema (Mirror DBs, Squad pages, Roadmap, PRDs, Delivery Pipeline). Deploy the 4 data-source workers for the 2 highest-value sources identified in Q2 kick-off. Validate fixtures against ground truth. Meet with EMs to understand current Monday workflow and calibrate the time-savings baseline. Meet with VP to confirm report format.

Week 2
May 27–31

Summarizers + first HITL loop

Deploy per-source Custom Agents. Run the first full pipeline on live data with EMs in the loop. Expect friction and corrections. Collect 4-6 weeks of previous reports from Arcline as golden-set inputs for the eval harness. The goal this week is not a good report; it's a working pipeline with visible errors the team can annotate.

Week 3
Jun 3–7

Master synthesizer + eval harness

Tune per-source agent prompts based on Week 2 EM rejection annotations. Deploy the master summarizer and run the first end-to-end pipeline against live Monday data. Backtest against previous reports; measure the initial Trust Score. Share with VP as a "preview" (not the official report yet; the manual process still runs in parallel). Target Trust Score ≥ 65% by end of week.

Week 4
Jun 10–14

Go live: parallel run, then primary

Run the AI pipeline as the primary Monday report on at least one Monday this week, with the manual process as a backup audit. Trust Score target: ≥ 75%. Present to VP alongside the manual report and ask: "Would you have made the same resource decisions from the AI version?" That answer (not the Trust Score) is the real POC gate. If yes: success. Plan the remaining 2 data sources for days 30–90.

Days 30–90

Remaining sources · confidence decay · on-demand queries

Add the remaining 2 data sources. Dial down HITL from 90 min to 45 min per squad as EM confidence grows; the eval harness provides the objective signal for when to reduce. By day 90: on-demand report generation (VP can request a mid-week update for a specific squad), and the HITL gate is optional rather than mandatory for squads with Trust Score ≥ 85% on 3 consecutive reports.

10 · Above and Beyond

Five additions that weren't in the prompt

🔍
PRD Fact Checker
A sixth summarizer agent that reads each squad's PRD database and flags drift between what the PRD says the feature should do and what the code actually shipped. It surfaces [BLOCK] reversals, like the Forge navigation decision where a Figma comment reversed a sprint-planning agreement already in the PRD. Most weekly digest tools ignore the PRD layer entirely; this makes PRD-code alignment a first-class signal in the VP report.
📊
Observability Dashboard
Every agent write goes through a shared writeAgentRunLog helper: run ID, agent name, start/end time, token cost, outcome. A live Notion dashboard (Agent Run Log + Weekly Pipeline Summary) shows exactly what ran, how long it took, and what it cost. Sprawl is the biggest risk in multi-agent systems; visibility is the mitigation. The dashboard makes pipeline health legible without a dedicated DevOps function.
🧪
Eval Harness with Golden-Set Replay
An LLM judge (Claude Haiku) samples every master report against a hand-authored ground-truth benchmark. The three-dimension Trust Score: citation coverage, source-fact alignment, backtest fidelity; it runs automatically after each report generation. A golden-set replay script exists specifically to catch prompt regressions in CI: if a prompt change causes the Trust Score to drop below the week's baseline, the script exits 1 and the change is blocked. Hallucination is measurable, not just hoped-against.
🎓
Teaching Layer
Every database and agent in the Notion workspace has a co-located explainer page: "What this is," "Who writes to it," "How to change the prompt." A top-level "How this workspace works" hub links everything and shows the architecture diagram. The goal: an Arcline engineer who wasn't in the room during the build can maintain and extend the system without Notion's help within 30 minutes of reading. Teaching the customer to fish is the only post-handoff support model that scales.

11b · How I Built This

Transparent process: a quiet pitch for how I work

This whole section is also an argument: I know how to wield AI assistants as a Solutions Engineer, not just be impressed by them.

1

Read the prompt, then did a Perplexity research pass

The research pass was strictly for grounding, surfacing how other teams have tackled multi-source weekly digests, what failure modes are typical, and what "trust in AI output" frameworks exist. The 5 questions, the V1 workflow design, the risks, and the 30-day plan in solution-intro.md are my own thinking, informed by that reading but not written by AI.

O

One Opus planning session expanded the solution into 17 self-contained PRDs

Single high-context session. It critiqued the hand-written solution doc, surfaced the approval-math problem (2/3 quorum risks publishing unreviewed squad content to VPs) and the hallucination-measurement gap, then broke the work into 17 PRDs with an explicit dependency graph for parallelization. The PRDs live in /prds. The planning session didn't write code: it authored work briefs.

S

Parallel Sonnet sessions executed individual PRDs in dependency waves

One session per PRD, scoped to a single brief and unable to see other sessions' working state. The PRDs are self-contained by design, so this works. Wave A (foundations), Wave B (7 parallel workers + observability + teaching), Wave C (summarizers + eval), Wave D (master), Wave E (this page). The git log mirrors the wave structure.

O

Opus review checkpoint after each wave

Between waves, an Opus session audited artifacts produced (workspace schema, fixtures, worker outputs, summarizer outputs, master report) against the next wave's PRDs and flagged contract drift before downstream sessions inherited it. Opus caught a quorum bug (the 2/3 → 100% approval change), a schema naming collision, and a citation contract gap in the fact-check agent, all before they compounded.

This approach works well for scoped fan-out work with clear contracts: each PRD is a well-defined brief that can execute independently. It falls down for ambiguous design work where context must be held across sessions; if a PRD's acceptance criteria are fuzzy, downstream sessions inherit the ambiguity multiplied. The PRD-authoring step is the load-bearing one. Treat it like code review, not project management.

  6. I need to be able to explain everything that is built.
  7. My ideas for the solution are listed below. I'm looking for additional innovation on top of my ideas. Go above and beyond, especially in the implementation phase.

Initial Questions

  1. In what format do the VPs tend to like to read the report, and how frequently are they making requests for updated content? Try to drive home at the final output and how frequently it is expected to change (i.e. flexibility).
  2. Which data source(s) has the most well-structured data? What about the least-well-structured? Which data source(s) make good candidates for a 30-day POC, or which should we leave off for deeper discovery?
  3. Which parts of the process take the most amount of time for the engineering managers? Time savings for that team is the core measurable KPI I'd like to pitch.
  4. What is the tolerance for human review gates in this solution? Would you accept human review in perpetuity, or is the overall goal to have a fully autonomous system? How much human-in-the-loop dependence can we have on the engineering managers for training, testing, and reducing the hallucination surface area?
  5. <What question am I missing here? What am I not asking?> (Answered in Section 02 above: Q5a on tool hygiene and Q5b on EM adoption risk.)

V1 Agentic Workflow

Data Source Workers: I'd like a worker to fire for each data source at 6 AM Monday morning. Each worker should have read access to each of their respective data sources, and read the last week of data into an associated database in Notion specifically scaffolded to the data source in question. I also want these workers to monitor for updates to their sources of truth throughout the process of creating the readout.

Data Source & Product Progress Summarizers: These are custom agents built within Notion that read updates since the last report and create a readout for the summarizer agent at the end of the workflow. One agent to also create is a "Product Roadmap Summarizer" and a "PRD Fact Checker." The reports these summarizer agents create should include an executive summary and citations back to the records they are using to generate insights inline on the Notion page. There should be an alert sent to a team of eng managers that will read the report and click an "Approved" button to create a HITL loop.

Master Summarizer Agent: This agent will be responsible for creating the final report once all specific data source summaries have been approved by eng managers. It will make sure to: generate the report in the format expected by VPs, cite sources back to the source of truth, incorporate feedback from previous EPD weekly documents, call out discrepancies between sources of truth within products, and call out areas of inefficiency or synergy between EPD teams.

2 Biggest Risks

Agent Sprawl: There's a lot going on here. I think asking the customer to manage all of this after a 30-day POC is going to be a lot to ask of them. We need to make sure we instrument the updates and validation of agent steps thoughtfully. The largest mitigation here is visibility. Make sure each engineering manager has their say in how they get their data displayed to them. This sprawl is a response to the potential hallucination concern brought by the customer in the problem statement. Creating many, smaller agents with reduced context is a way to avoid hallucination risk by overflooding context windows. I want to explicitly call this out as this problem seems entirely self-inflicted, but it's for a very good reason.

Data Staleness: There really isn't any way to deal with this technically if there is already concern that 3 to 4 hours is already too slow. We just need to explicitly call out via the master summary agent when data is stale at the time of report creation, and then call out that the staleness problem will be dealt with once we are confident in the initial report generation capabilities.

30-Day POC

Week 1: Build the data workers and mirror copy databases as well as the product-specific summarizer agents. This includes any additional scaffolding like tracking report progress and the summary databases. We will meet regularly with eng managers to understand how they will want to be alerted for their input on a summary agent, and meet with VPs regularly to understand success criteria and the final output report structure.

Week 2: Start to build the data-product-specific agents and actually implement HITL loops with eng managers for their specific workflows. Also start to build the master summary agent at this time, and collect 2 months worth of previous readouts to test against.

Week 3: Finalize the product-data-specific outputs with the engineering managers. Build the master summary agent and those loops as well based on feedback from eng managers. Start to backchannel initial results up to the VPs. We need to be running our first full test of the pipeline by the end of week 3, backtesting against reports we pulled in week 2.

Week 4: Backtesting against previous reports continues, including a live run against the weekly report on Monday. We tweak around the edges of the pipeline to get to feature parity with that report for the specific data sources identified in the POC scope before we kicked off.

Success criteria: We have built confidence that the agent-generated report meets the standards to hand off to VPs. The fully agentic pipeline should take a little more time, but it's important to build the confidence more than anything at this stage. I believe having a report out by 11 AM should be the minimum acceptable bar. Internally and with the eng managers, we will target 10 AM.

Days 30-90: Quickly scaffold out the remaining data sources to get full context to the summarizer agents. Once we have all the data sources, we can dial in the HITL feedback mechanism. By 90 days, we should be able to generate ad hoc / daily reports for VPs as needed.
