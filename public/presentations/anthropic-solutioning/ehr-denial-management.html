Customer Challenge

No first-class
claim object

A top-5 ambulatory EHR/PM vendor evaluating 20+ AI vendors. One medical group as the proving ground: ~20,000 claims/month, ~14,000 workable denials. The first discovery: there is no stored claim object in the platform.

Discovery The platform has no first-class claim object. The lifetime of a claim had to be reconstructed by stitching encounters together with medical records and 835 remittance data. A denial is a derived state: a zero-dollar 835 line with a CARC/RARC code, not a stored entity.
Data Gap The obvious data source: an 18-19 file daily pipe-delimited feed. It had no line-item detail. The 16-person AR team spent 2 hours each morning consolidating files manually. The data we needed required a different source entirely.
Approach 5 SME sessions before any build. Full map of the data model: encounter to 835 to line-item report. The discovery work was the product: understanding this unlocks reuse across ~80 managed-services customers.

Appendix A

Claim Lifecycle
Reconstruction

Why the daily feed was the wrong source, and how stitching three data layers produces the claim object the platform doesn't store. This is the insight that makes platform-wide reuse possible.

Layer 1: Encounter The clinical record: patient, provider, date of service, procedure codes. This is the source of truth for what happened. Claims are regenerated from encounters: they are never edited, which is why there is no "claim" to update on denial.
Layer 2: 835 remittance The payer's response: payment amounts, zero-dollar lines, CARC/RARC denial codes. The 835 joined to the encounter produces the denial record. The daily feed omitted this join entirely: only the line-item report contained it.
Layer 3: Line-item report Charge-level detail: service line breakdowns, payer adjustments, contractual write-offs. This is the source the AR team consolidated manually each morning. Stitching all three layers creates the claim view that drives the LLM investigation engine.

Appendix B

Platform
Scale Model

Why this is a platform deal, not a single-customer integration. The reconstruction logic learned from one medical group applies across ~80 managed-services customers sharing the same EHR/PM schema.

Platform breadth 3,000-customer platform. ~80 managed-services customers share a substantially similar EHR/PM configuration. The claim reconstruction layer is schema-level work: once proven, the same 835 + encounter + line-item join applies across the entire managed-services base.
Customization surface Per-customer variation: payer mix, routing rules, exception thresholds, write-back timing. The core reconstruction logic is shared. The 5-session SME bootcamp compounds: platform knowledge acquired once, deployed everywhere.
Selection rationale Chosen over 20+ competing AI vendors. The differentiator was not the LLM: it was the data model work. Most vendors assumed a denial object existed and built on that assumption. We found the real data first.
