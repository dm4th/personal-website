# AirOps — AI Solutions Architect

### What Drove the Score

Strong on core job function (9/10) and technical skills (9/10): the build-internally-productize-externally motion at Smarter Technologies is a near-exact match for what the role describes. Technical depth in LLMs, RAG, Python, and workflow automation is all documented at production level. ### Strongest Talking Points

**1. The productization motion is the job.**
"At Smarter Technologies I built a 22-skill AI platform using Claude Code, then ported the highest-impact skills to a shared co-work interface so non-technical teammates could run them without writing a single prompt. That internal-to-external productization motion is what I understand AirOps to be describing: build it until it works, then make it repeatable for the people who need it."

**2. The Value Clicks arc.**
"At Action Network I built the Value Clicks model — a probability-weighted conversion attribution score for every on-page user action. The model itself was interesting. What it enabled was more interesting. The entire editorial org aligned their roadmaps around it. I met weekly with the CCO to translate data into priorities, worked with individual editors on content mix, and coached individual writers on SEO structure. The company grew affiliate revenue 270% in the 12 months after launch. That was a data-to-decision translation exercise, not a modeling exercise."

**3. Production AI is different from demo AI.**
"My hybrid-RAG eligibility engine at Thoughtful hit 95% accuracy within two days on a live workflow. I had labeled holdout datasets, precision and recall tracking by failure category, and regression tests before every model or prompt change. The eval infrastructure was the reason accuracy held. My design principle: use AI where it genuinely adds value, write deterministic code everywhere else. That is what separates systems that work in production from systems that work in demos."

**4. The retention signal.**
"I was retained through an 85% workforce reduction at Smarter Technologies. The reason, as explained to me, was a specific skill: I can take genuinely complex AI systems and make them legible to the people who need to adopt them. Not dumbed down. Connected to their actual workflows, in language that gives them confidence to move. That is what I think the Solutions Architect role at AirOps is fundamentally about."

**5. The feedback loop architecture.**
"I built hooks-based feedback sampling into the AI platform: every skill invocation was logged, 15-20% of runs prompted for thumbs-up/down ratings, and that signal routed directly back into skill specification iteration. A cluster of thumbs-down responses with notes about 'wrong context' told me exactly what to fix. That feedback loop architecture is something I would want to bring to every workflow I build at AirOps."

### Remaining Gaps and How to Address Them

**Gap 1: No marketing/growth-team buyer experience**
AirOps's ICP is marketing and growth orgs at B2B SaaS companies. Dan has sold to and built for healthcare enterprise buyers almost exclusively.

*How to address:* Be direct. "The specific buyer persona is new to me. The motion is not: I understand how to translate AI workflow value to non-technical decision-makers, how to scope what will and won't be built, and how to close the feedback loop from usage back to iteration. I learn verticals fast. I'd expect a short domain ramp, not a career change."

**Gap 2: No Zapier by name**
JD uses Zapier as the primary workflow automation example.

*How to address:* "I've rebuilt broken Make automations in production clinical contexts and built bidirectional webhook integrations between Make and Python AI agents. Zapier and Make share the same trigger-action-router architecture. I'd be productive in Zapier within a day. I'd rather show you what I can build in it than describe what I know about it."

**Gap 3: Seniority band mismatch**
JD targets 2-6 years. Dan has ~9 years with Director and VP titles. May create comp expectation concerns.

*How to address:* "I'm drawn to this role specifically because it's hands-on building, not because of title. I've spent my whole career preferring to build over managing. The titles followed the output, not the other way around. What matters to me is being close to customers and close to the product, and that is what this role offers."

### Questions to Ask in the Interview

1. "What does the first workflow look like — the first thing the Solutions Architect would build internally and then productize for customers? How does that prioritization decision get made?"

2. "The JD describes monitoring key metrics and reporting findings to the exec team. What are the metrics you care most about right now: usage rates, template adoption, customer time-to-value? What's the reporting cadence?"

3. "You've 5x'd revenue in the last year. What's the biggest constraint on continued growth — is it template coverage across verticals, platform capability gaps, or something on the customer education/adoption side?"

4. "How does the feedback loop from customer usage back into template iteration actually work today? Is there a structured process or is it more ad hoc?"

5. "AirOps is betting that AI search (ChatGPT, Perplexity, AI Overviews) is becoming the primary discovery channel for branded content. How far along are your customers in making that shift, and where do they most need help?"
