## Strongest Talking Points

1. **The manifest-as-documentation principle.** When I built the 22-skill Cowork platform, the design constraint I held from day one was that the enablement artifact and the platform artifact had to be the same object. Every YAML manifest was simultaneously the configuration spec for the skill and the onboarding document for the person using it. That's not a documentation strategy bolted on afterward. It's a design discipline. If you want a feedback loop that actually improves content over time, you have to start there.

2. **The feedback loop instrumentation story.** I implemented pre/post tool-use hooks to sample 15-20% of skill invocations for thumbs-up/down ratings with optional notes. Within three weeks I had a 93% approval rating, but more importantly, I had signal: a cluster of thumbs-down on the pricing skill with notes about wrong EHR context told me exactly which part of the skill spec needed to be updated. That's how I'd approach measuring curriculum effectiveness here. Not surveys. Signal from the field, at the moment of use.

3. **The "when they started Slacking me to query my agent" inflection point.** There was a specific moment at Smarter Technologies where I knew the adoption barrier was real: team members started Slacking me to ask my agent questions on their behalf because the prompting overhead felt too high. My response was to redesign the natural language entry points to eliminate that barrier and document the pattern as a reusable deployment framework. That's the problem this role exists to solve at Anthropic's scale, and I've already solved it once.

4. **Making AI legible drives outcomes, not just adoption.** At Thoughtful AI, the analytics product I built wasn't just about internal reporting. Once the CEO and CPO saw how those dashboards translated agent execution logs into terms a healthcare CFO could act on, they brought me into every large deal. 96% of those prospects moved to proposal within a month. I wasn't a better closer. I was better at making the technology legible in the context of the specific workflow the customer was trying to fix. That's the core skill this role depends on.

5. **The teaching pattern runs through the whole career, not just the AI work.** SQL courses at FanDuel, written from scratch to match the operations team's specific reporting workflows. Google Apps Script training at Google, delivered alongside the automation so the team could maintain it independently. Demo coaching rubrics for SE candidates at Smarter Technologies, evaluating technical communication against real prospect scenarios. Every technical role has had a "how do I make this learnable" layer. That's not something I developed for this application. It's just how I work.

In conversation: be direct about this. The motion is the same, the audience is different. All of it has been written for a specific reader trying to make a specific decision. None of it is public. In conversation: acknowledge this directly and express genuine interest in building that external presence as part of this role. The communication muscle is there. The format and audience are new. **Target audience is software engineers, not GTM teams.** My entire career has involved making AI legible to non-technical enterprise buyers and operators. The target ICP for this role is developers and engineering teams. In conversation: the analytical rigor goes up, the translation burden goes down. Teaching developers is a different motion but not a harder one. What changes is the level of technical depth you can assume and the kind of credibility signals that matter. My production Claude Code platform is the credibility signal. I'd lead with it.
