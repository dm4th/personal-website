# The Search Process

When the time came to choose a college, I initially wanted to explore options beyond my parents' alma mater, [Bucknell](https://www.bucknell.edu/). However, as I visited various campuses with my family, I couldn't help but compare everything to Bucknell—which had always been my benchmark for what a college should look like. The combination of a strong engineering program and a robust liberal arts college made the decision a no-brainer after some honest reflection.

# Picking a Major

I arrived on campus as an undeclared engineering major, which was a common path at Bucknell due to the required introductory course, Engineering 100. All first-semester engineering students took four courses together: Calculus, Physics I, a Foundation Seminar, and ENG 100.

ENG 100 rotated students through three engineering disciplines over nine weeks—Biomedical, Electrical, and Computer Science. All three were fascinating, but Computer Science and Electrical Engineering stood out. Rather than choosing between them immediately, I discovered the [Computer Engineering major](https://coursecatalog.bucknell.edu/collegeofengineeringcurricula/areasofstudy/computerengineeringcpeg/#majortext), which covered elements of both. The plan was to pursue it until the end of sophomore year, then decide whether to specialize in software or hardware.

I never switched.

# Computer Engineering

The Computer Engineering program at Bucknell was genuinely exceptional. The major had only six students total when I was there—the smallest program in the College of Engineering—which meant a lot of direct faculty attention and a tight cohort. The curriculum covered everything from understanding electrical signals at the hardware level to higher-level abstractions like data structures and algorithms. By graduation I had a comprehensive understanding of how computers actually worked, which turned out to be a better foundation than I realized at the time.

The range of topics—circuits, operating systems, assembly language, software engineering, networking—gave me the mental models to move between hardware and software contexts. That flexibility has been useful throughout my career in ways I didn't anticipate.
